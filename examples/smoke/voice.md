# Initial Voice Profile

This is a starter voice profile. Copy it to your consuming project's root as `voice.md` and rewrite it for your project — the pipeline's voice-consuming steps (drafting, prose pass, line pass, metaphor workshop) read the project-root `voice.md`, not this template. `install.sh` copies this file to the project root on first install. Treat the project-specific names below as placeholders.

## The job of the prose

The prose is not the experience. The characters and the world are the experience.
Prose that calls attention to itself has failed. The reader should never surface
to notice a sentence — they should be pulled forward through it into what is
happening to Louise.

The target feeling: silk sheets, or coasting downhill. Forward momentum that
makes reading feel effortless, so the weight of the story lands on the story,
not on the language carrying it.

## Sentence rhythm

Default to sentences with subordination, but subordination that moves forward
into precision — not sideways into qualification. A subordinate clause earns its
place by adding a specific sensory, psychological, or causal detail. It does not
earn its place by restating the main clause with different words, hedging, or
second-guessing with constructions like "or rather" or "that is to say."

If a clause could be cut and the sentence would be more accurate without it,
cut it.

Short sentences are punctuation, not texture. Use them to land a beat, not to
build rhythm.

## Detail

Every physical detail must pass one of two tests:
- It reveals something about Louise's state in this moment
- It deepens the dread or institutional texture of the scene

Cut detail that only populates the world. The reader is not here to see the room.
They are here to feel what the room is doing to Louise.

## Point of view

This is Louise's perception, filtered close. The perceiving mind is thirteen years
old. Reference points, comparisons, and the texture of interiority come from her
world, not an adult intellectual's. The sentences may be syntactically complex;
the consciousness behind them is not.

The narrator runs slightly ahead of Louise's conscious acknowledgment. She
perceives before she understands. The prose may register wrongness before Louise
has named it. It may not explain what she has not yet explained to herself.

## Emotion

Do not name grief, dread, or exhaustion directly unless Louise is actively
labeling her own state out loud. Arrive at emotion through physical perception,
selective attention, and the weight of the sentence — not through the word for
the feeling.

## What this is not

Not romance register. Not comedic. Not action-thriller pacing. Not world-painter
detail. Not Hemingway. Not prose that wants to be noticed.
